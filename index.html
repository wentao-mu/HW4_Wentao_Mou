How a Small Number of States Decides U.S. Presidential Elections

National presidential results can look stable from year to year, but the coalition that produces a win is much more geographic. Scroll down to move from the national vote, to the closest 2020 battlegrounds, to the states that realigned over time, and finally to the regional structure of the 2020 winning map.

01

The national vote usually stays tight

Looking only at the national two-party vote, most elections since 1976 stayed within a fairly narrow band. A few cycles were clear wins, but many of them were decided by only a few points once the vote is collapsed into one national number.

02

2020 depended on a handful of close states

The 2020 result did not hinge on every state equally. Georgia, Arizona, Wisconsin, and Pennsylvania were all extremely close, which means a modest national edge was turned into a winning map through a very small set of battlegrounds.

03

Realignment came from specific places

Comparing 2008 to 2020 shows that states did not move together. Arizona and Georgia shifted toward Democrats, while Iowa, Ohio, and West Virginia moved sharply toward Republicans, showing that the national story hides very different regional paths.

04

The winning coalitions were regional

The 2020 treemap groups winning states first by party and then by region. Democratic winning votes are concentrated in large coastal and metro-heavy states plus a few flips, while Republican winning votes are spread across the South and interior.
